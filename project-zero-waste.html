Circular Economy · ISM-MICMAC

Evaluation of Barriers to Zero Waste in the Automotive Industry

University of Tehran · Summer 2022

Overview

As global industries face the imperative of environmental sustainability, the automotive sector faces significant challenges in managing waste generation. This project addresses the critical transition from a "Linear Economy" (take-make-dispose) to a "Circular Economy" (reduce-reuse-recycle).

The study focuses on evaluating the barriers impeding the implementation of Zero Waste management systems. By identifying 14 specific obstacles—ranging from technological limitations to regulatory gaps—this research provides a structural model to assist decision-makers in prioritizing interventions for achieving a closed-loop supply chain.

Methodology

The research adopted a multi-phase quantitative and qualitative approach to analyze the complex interrelationships among barriers. The methodology proceeded in four distinct stages:

  • Barrier Identification: A comprehensive literature review identified 14 key barriers to Zero Waste adoption.
  • Expert Validation: A panel of industry experts from SAIPA Corporation (including process design and logistics specialists) validated the relevance of these barriers.
  • Interpretive Structural Modeling (ISM): A Structural Self-Interaction Matrix (SSIM) was developed to map the contextual relationships between barriers, resulting in an 8-level hierarchical model.
  • MICMAC Analysis: Barriers were classified into four clusters (Autonomous, Dependent, Linkage, and Independent) based on their driving power and dependence power to identify root causes.

Key Findings

The structural analysis revealed that the barriers to Zero Waste are not isolated but interconnected in a hierarchical system. The key academic findings include:

  • Root Causes (Independent Variables): The study identified "Lack of government support" (B13) and "Financial constraints" (B3) as the foundational barriers. These variables possess high driving power, influencing all other levels of the system.
  • Linkage Variables: "Technological difficulties" (B2) emerged as a linkage variable, indicating it is an unstable element that is both highly influential and highly sensitive to other factors.
  • Dependent Outcomes: Issues such as "Lack of supply chain integration" (B9) and "Lack of public interest" (B6) were identified as Level 1 dependent variables—meaning they are symptoms resulting from the unresolved foundational issues.
